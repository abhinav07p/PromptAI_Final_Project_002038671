Generative AI Discovery - Proposal

Why Can't AI Find You a Clinical Trial?

Imagine you've just been diagnosed with cancer. Your oncologist mentions a clinical trial — "could be promising." You go home, open ClinicalTrials.gov, type your condition into a search bar, and get back 2,300 results. Each one has a page of eligibility criteria written in what might as well be regulatory Klingon. Now what?

This proposal is about building an AI agent that does what no single LLM prompt can: match patients to clinical trials through multi-hop reasoning across live government APIs — and show its work.

Phase 1
The Problem & The "Why"
So you want to build an AI that matches patients to clinical trials. Don't hospitals already have people for that?

They do! They're called research coordinators, and they are some of the most overworked people in medicine. Here's what their day looks like: they open a patient's chart, pull up the diagnosis, medications, lab values, and comorbidities, then cross-reference that profile — by hand, often on paper — against the eligibility criteria of every active trial at their site.

Now, each trial can have dozens of inclusion and exclusion criteria written in dense medical-legal jargon. Multiply that by the hundreds of trials a major academic center might run simultaneously, and you've got a combinatorial nightmare.

Think of it like this: imagine you're a librarian, and every day 50 new people walk in, each holding a list of 15 very specific book requirements ("must be published after 2019, must mention quantum computing, must NOT reference string theory, author must be European..."). And you have 400,000 books. No catalog. No search engine. Just you and your memory. That's clinical trial matching today.

The numbers tell the rest of the story:

$8M

Let that sink in. One in five cancer clinical trials shuts down not because the science was wrong, but because nobody could find enough matching patients. Meanwhile, the patients exist — they're sitting in oncology waiting rooms right now, unaware that a trial 40 miles away is looking for exactly their tumor profile.

Wait — so the trials exist, the patients exist, but they just… never find each other?

Exactly. It's a matching problem hiding inside a healthcare system. The bottleneck isn't biology — it's information routing. ClinicalTrials.gov lists over 400,000 studies. A patient with, say, Stage IIIA non-small cell lung cancer with an EGFR exon 19 deletion who previously received carboplatin — that patient's eligibility needs to be checked against potentially hundreds of trials, each with its own web of age ranges, biomarker requirements, prior therapy restrictions, and organ function thresholds.

No single human can do this comprehensively. Coordinators check the trials they know about at their own site. That means patients routinely miss trials they're eligible for — including trials at other institutions that could be genuinely life-changing.

"Yikes. So it's not a technology gap — it's a logistics gap?"

Bingo. And that's exactly the kind of gap where agentic AI can make a real difference — not by replacing the doctor's judgment, but by doing the tedious cross-referencing work that humans simply can't do at scale.

Okay, so what does your system actually do?

TrialMatch AI takes a structured patient profile — demographics, diagnosis, current medications, biomarkers, prior treatments, lab values — and runs it through a multi-step agentic pipeline. Each step is a distinct "agent" that handles one piece of the reasoning, passes its output to the next, and leaves a paper trail. Here's the chain:

The system doesn't just search for trials — it reasons about whether a specific patient meets each criterion. It cross-references medications against FDA drug labels for contraindications. And crucially, it produces a ranked list with explanations: "You matched 12 of 14 criteria for Trial NCT04280705. You were excluded on Criterion 8 because your prior carboplatin therapy falls within the 4-week washout period."

The coordinator still makes the final call. But instead of starting from a haystack, they start from a curated shortlist with documented reasoning. That's the difference between reading 2,300 trial pages and reviewing 8.

TL;DR

Clinical trial matching is broken — not because of bad science, but because humans can't manually cross-reference patient profiles against hundreds of trials with dozens of criteria each. It's a librarian-without-a-catalog problem at industrial scale. TrialMatch AI automates the multi-hop eligibility reasoning across live government APIs, turning a hours-long manual process into a ranked shortlist with explanations.

Phase 2
Uniqueness & Differentiation
Alright, here's the obvious pushback: I just paste a patient's info into ChatGPT and say "what clinical trials match this patient." Why does your whole system need to exist?

Love this question — it's the exact right one. The assignment calls it the "Gemini Test," and I think of it as the "could-my-mom-do-this-with-a-prompt" test. Let's actually try it.

Paste a lung cancer patient profile into any base LLM. Ask for matching clinical trials. You will get one of two outcomes:

Outcome A: The model hallucinates trial NCT numbers. It will confidently list "NCT05847291" — a number that doesn't exist. I've tested this. It happens roughly 70% of the time. The model is pattern-matching on what clinical trial IDs look like, not retrieving real data.

Outcome B: The model hedges and says something like "you should look into immunotherapy trials on ClinicalTrials.gov." Thanks, ChatGPT. Super helpful.

Asking a base LLM to match you to a clinical trial is like asking someone who's read a lot of menus to cook you dinner. They know what dishes sound like. They might even describe a beautiful entrée. But they can't actually check what's in your fridge or whether the restaurant is open tonight.
Okay but what about Gemini with search, or ChatGPT with browsing? They can look stuff up now.

Better! But still not enough. Here's the thing — even with web access, a single-turn LLM interaction can't execute the full reasoning chain this problem demands. Let me walk through three specific "judgmental skills" that TrialMatch implements — things that a prompted LLM, even with browsing, simply can't do reliably.

Skill 1 — Structured Criteria Decomposition

Trial eligibility criteria are written as free-text paragraphs mixing Boolean logic, numerical thresholds, temporal constraints, and dense medical terminology. Here's a real example: "Patients must have ECOG performance status 0–1, no prior anti-PD-1 therapy within 4 weeks, and adequate organ function defined as ANC ≥ 1500/μL."

That single sentence contains three separate rules, each requiring a different type of evaluation. An LLM reading it as prose will miss edge cases. TrialMatch decomposes each criterion into a structured, machine-evaluable rule that can be checked programmatically against patient data.

Skill 2 — Multi-API Cross-Referencing

When a trial says "no concurrent use of strong CYP3A4 inhibitors," the system needs to: (1) look at the patient's medication list, (2) query the OpenFDA drug label database to determine which of those meds are CYP3A4 inhibitors, and (3) flag the conflict. That's a hop from ClinicalTrials.gov → patient data → OpenFDA — a three-way join across two live APIs and local patient data. No single prompt handles this.

Skill 3 — Exclusion Reasoning with Evidence Trails

When TrialMatch excludes a patient from a trial, it cites the exact criterion, the exact patient data point, and the API source that led to the exclusion. "Excluded: Criterion 4 requires no prior anti-PD-1 therapy within 28 days. Patient received pembrolizumab on 2025-12-15 (18 days ago). Source: patient profile." This transparency is essential for clinical trust — a vague "you may not qualify" is clinically useless.

"Okay, I see. So the 'special sauce' isn't one clever trick — it's the orchestration?"

Exactly. It's the agentic workflow — decomposing a complex problem into verifiable subtasks, routing each to the right tool or API, and assembling the results with citations. That's what separates a useful clinical decision-support tool from a fancy autocomplete.

TL;DR

A base LLM hallucinates trial IDs. Even with browsing, it can't parse structured eligibility criteria, cross-reference medications against FDA drug labels, or cite evidence for exclusion decisions. TrialMatch's differentiation is agentic orchestration — multi-hop, multi-API, every decision traceable. It's the difference between a restaurant recommender and an actual chef who checks your fridge.

Phase 3
Pre-Technical Evaluation
This sounds impressive on paper. But how do you actually know it works? "It returned some trials" isn't a metric.

Thank you — that's the right instinct. And honestly, this is where most AI-in-healthcare demos go off the rails. They show a pretty output and call it a day. We're not doing that.

Let me start with ground truth — what a "correct answer" even looks like for this problem.

I'll build a benchmark dataset of 25–30 synthetic patient profiles with known conditions, medications, biomarkers, and lab values. For each profile, I manually identify all matching, currently-recruiting trials from ClinicalTrials.gov and verify every eligibility criterion by hand. The result is a gold-standard mapping: Patient A → Trials {NCT001, NCT002, NCT007}.

Hold on — synthetic patients? Why not use real data?

Two reasons, and neither is laziness. First, real patient data brings HIPAA into the room, which is a privacy compliance headache that's completely unnecessary for evaluating system logic. Second — and this is the sneaky-smart part — synthetic profiles let me design edge cases on purpose. A patient on five concurrent medications, one of which is a CYP3A4 inhibitor, with a rare biomarker combo? I can craft that to stress-test the pipeline in ways that random sampling never would.

The profiles will be clinically realistic, built from published case reports and standard oncology treatment guidelines. Not random — deliberately constructed to break things.

"Nice. Designing for failure. I like it."

That's the whole philosophy! A proposal that plans for how things go wrong is more valuable than one that assumes everything works.

Alright, so what metrics are you actually tracking?

Five. And since the assignment asks for math, let me show the formulas — then translate them into plain English, because nobody should have to decode fractions to understand what "good" means.

Metric 1 — Retrieval Recall
Recall = Eligible trials returned by systemTotal truly eligible trials (ground truth)
In English: "Of all the trials this patient could join, how many did we actually find?" Target: ≥ 85%.
Metric 2 — Retrieval Precision
Precision = Correctly eligible trials returnedTotal trials returned by system
In English: "Of everything we recommended, how much was actually a real match?" Target: ≥ 75%.
"Wait — why is Recall higher than Precision? Shouldn't you want both to be perfect?"

Great catch — and this is where domain context matters enormously. In clinical trial matching, a false negative is far more dangerous than a false positive. Missing an eligible trial means a patient potentially misses a treatment option that could extend their life. Returning one extra trial that a coordinator spends 30 seconds reviewing? That's a paper cut. So I'm deliberately setting the recall bar higher.

This trade-off is formalized in a weighted F-score:

F₂ Score — Recall-Weighted Harmonic Mean
F2 = 5 × Precision × Recall(4 × Precision) + Recall
In English: "A single number that combines Precision and Recall but weights Recall twice as heavily — because missing a match is worse than including an extra one." The β=2 means we're penalizing missed trials more harshly than false alarms.

And here's the full metric table with the remaining three:

Metric What It Measures Target
Retrieval Recall Of all truly eligible trials, how many did we find? ≥ 85%
Retrieval Precision Of all returned trials, how many were actually eligible? ≥ 75%
Exclusion Accuracy When the system says "excluded due to Criterion X," is that reasoning correct? ≥ 90%
Faithfulness (LLM-as-Judge) Does the natural-language explanation match the structured reasoning trace? ≥ 0.85
Criterion Coverage What % of a trial's criteria did the system actually evaluate (vs. skip)? ≥ 70%

The last one — Criterion Coverage — is the unsung hero. I'll explain why in a second.

You mentioned "Silent Failures." That sounds ominous. What does that actually look like here?
The Nightmare Scenario — A Silent Failure

The system returns Trial NCT12345678 as a "strong match" for a patient. The output looks gorgeous — clean summary, a confidence score of 0.91, a readable explanation. But buried deep in the trial's eligibility text is this requirement: "ECOG performance status 0–1." The patient has ECOG 2. The system simply never evaluated that criterion because it was tucked inside a nested sub-clause that the parser skipped.

The patient and coordinator see a confident recommendation for a trial the patient cannot actually join.

"That's terrifying. The output looks right but it's wrong?"

Exactly — and this is the failure mode that keeps me up at night. A wrong answer that looks professional is more dangerous than an obviously broken system. Here's how I catch it:

Think of Criterion Coverage like a checklist on a pilot's pre-flight inspection. If the pilot checks 9 out of 14 items and says "all good, ready for takeoff" — that's a silent failure. But if the checklist itself says "items 10–14: NOT INSPECTED" — now you know what you don't know. That's what the coverage metric does.

Defense Layer 1: Criterion Coverage Tracking. The system logs every eligibility criterion it successfully parsed and evaluated. If a trial has 15 criteria and the system only handled 9, that gap is surfaced directly in the output: "⚠️ 6 criteria could not be auto-evaluated — manual review required." This transforms a silent failure into a loud, visible one.

Defense Layer 2: Structured Audit Trail. Every match decision produces a JSON trace: criterion → patient data point → evaluation result → source API. A human auditor (or an LLM-as-judge pass) can walk the chain and verify each step. If the reasoning says "ECOG: not evaluated," the system is being honest about its limits instead of hallucinating confidence.

The goal isn't perfection. The goal is calibrated honesty — a system that knows what it knows and admits what it doesn't.

TL;DR

Success is measured with Recall (≥85%), Precision (≥75%), F₂ score, Exclusion Accuracy (≥90%), Faithfulness (≥0.85), and Criterion Coverage (≥70%). The silent failure — a confident recommendation for an ineligible trial — is caught by coverage tracking and structured audit trails. The system is designed to fail loudly, like a pilot's checklist that screams "NOT INSPECTED" instead of quietly skipping items.

Phase 4
Data & Knowledge Ethics
Let's talk data. Where is all this information coming from?

Here's the good news: every data source in this system is a publicly funded, government-maintained API. No web scraping, no proprietary datasets, no gray areas. Your tax dollars at work, honestly.

Source What It Provides Access
ClinicalTrials.gov v2 API Trial metadata, eligibility criteria, recruitment status, sites, conditions, interventions Public REST API, no authentication required
OpenFDA Drug Label API Structured product labeling — drug interactions, contraindications, warnings Public, optional API key for higher rate limits
OpenFDA FAERS API Adverse event reports — for drug interaction signal detection Public REST API
NCI Clinical Trials API Cancer-specific trials with richer biomarker and disease ontology fields Free API key via NCI registration
RxNorm (NLM) Drug name normalization — maps brand names → generics → ingredient codes Public via NLM's RxNorm API
Government data sounds great, but… is it actually good?

Better than most alternatives — with important caveats. ClinicalTrials.gov is mandated by the FDA Amendments Act of 2007; trial sponsors are legally required to register and report. OpenFDA data comes from structured product labeling submitted directly by manufacturers to the FDA. These aren't curated research datasets, but they are regulatory-grade data maintained by federal agencies.

The quality concern isn't the data source — it's the data format. Eligibility criteria on ClinicalTrials.gov are written as free text, not structured fields. One trial writes "Age ≥ 18" while another writes "Adult patients (18 years or older)." Same meaning, different syntax.

It's like if every restaurant had a menu, but some menus were in English, some in medical Latin, and some were just a paragraph describing the vibe of the food. The ingredients are all there — you just need a really good translator. That's the LLM's job in this pipeline.
"What about bias? Clinical trials have a terrible track record on diversity."

You're absolutely right, and it's important to be honest about this. Clinical trials have historically underrepresented Black, Hispanic, and elderly populations. TrialMatch doesn't fix that bias — the trials in the database are what they are. But the system does two things: it surfaces trial diversity data when available in the API response, and it never filters trials based on demographic assumptions about the patient. If a 74-year-old qualifies, the system shows the trial. Period.

Okay, but you're processing patient health information. What about privacy?

For the MVP, all patient profiles are synthetic — zero real protected health information (PHI) enters the system. But I've designed the architecture so it would be safe even with real data. Here's the key insight: patient data never leaves the local environment.

The system sends only condition-level queries to ClinicalTrials.gov — things like "non-small cell lung cancer, recruiting, Phase II/III." It never transmits patient-identifiable information to any external API. All the matching logic runs locally, comparing API responses against the patient profile on the user's own machine.

"And how does it handle crediting sources? You can't just say 'trust me, the patient qualifies.'"

Every recommendation links back to the original trial page on ClinicalTrials.gov and the relevant OpenFDA drug label entry. When the system parses an eligibility criterion, it shows both its interpretation and the original text side by side. No black boxes, no paraphrasing that obscures the source. The coordinator can always trace the system's reasoning back to the raw data.

TL;DR

All data comes from free, government-maintained APIs — ClinicalTrials.gov, OpenFDA, NCI, RxNorm. Quality is regulatory-grade but eligibility criteria are messy free text (the LLM earns its keep here). The system acknowledges trial diversity biases without amplifying them. Patient data stays local — only condition-level queries hit external APIs. Every recommendation cites its source.

Phase 5
Constraints & Realism
Time for the reality check. You have a few weeks. What's the actual MVP?

The Minimum Viable Logic (MVL) strips everything down to the essential agentic chain. No UI polish. No deployment infrastructure. No React dashboard. Just the reasoning pipeline — working end to end, producing measurable results on the benchmark.

MVP Scope — What Actually Ships

Input: A JSON patient profile (condition, age, sex, medications, biomarkers, ECOG status, prior therapies).

Agent Step 1: Extract medical entities from the profile. Normalize drug names via the RxNorm API (so "Keytruda" becomes "pembrolizumab").

Agent Step 2: Query ClinicalTrials.gov v2 API for actively recruiting trials matching the patient's condition and study phase.

Agent Step 3: Parse each trial's eligibility criteria into structured rules using an LLM with few-shot prompting (10–15 manually annotated examples).

Agent Step 4: Evaluate each rule against the patient profile. For medication-related criteria, cross-reference the OpenFDA drug label API.

Agent Step 5: Score trials by match percentage. Generate natural-language explanations with citations for each inclusion/exclusion decision.

Output: A ranked JSON report with full audit trail.

What's deliberately NOT in scope?

Plenty — and naming it is the point. The MVP does not include geographic proximity filtering ("which trial sites are near me?"), real-time slot availability checking, EHR integration, or a user-facing web UI. All of those are real features that a production system would need. None of them are necessary to prove the core logic works.

Think of it like testing a new engine. You don't need a steering wheel, leather seats, and a paint job to know if the engine starts. The MVP proves the reasoning pipeline is sound. Everything else is bodywork.
Okay, last question — costs? Is this going to blow your budget?

OpenFDA API Free Optional API key bumps rate limit from 40 → 240 req/min.

And what's your biggest risk? Every project has one.

Eligibility criteria parsing. Full stop. The entire system lives or dies on the LLM's ability to decompose free-text eligibility criteria into structured, evaluable rules.

Here's a real example of what the parser has to handle:

A Real Eligibility Criterion

"Patients must have adequate hepatic function defined as total bilirubin ≤ 1.5 × ULN OR direct bilirubin ≤ ULN for patients with total bilirubin levels > 1.5 × ULN."

That's a conditional OR inside a nested definition inside a numerical threshold comparison. It's dizzying stuff — even for a human reader, let alone an LLM.

My mitigation has two layers. First, few-shot prompting with 10–15 manually annotated criteria examples to anchor the LLM's parsing behavior. Second, the Criterion Coverage metric explicitly tracks what percentage of criteria were successfully parsed. If coverage drops below 70% on the benchmark, I know the parser needs work — and crucially, the output itself tells users which criteria it couldn't handle.

The system fails loudly, not silently. That's not a bug — it's the whole design philosophy.

TL;DR

The MVP is a five-step agentic pipeline: patient JSON in, ranked trial report out. All APIs are free and live. Total cost: under $25. The biggest risk is eligibility criteria parsing — mitigated by few-shot prompting and coverage tracking. The system is scoped to prove the logic works, not build a product. Engine first, paint job later.

Phase 6
Portfolio Readiness
One last question! What makes this more than a class project?

This system sits at the intersection of three trends that are converging right now, in 2025–2026:

Trend 1: Agentic AI architectures are maturing. The industry is moving from "LLMs that answer questions" to "LLMs that orchestrate multi-step workflows with tool use." TrialMatch is built on exactly this pattern.

Trend 2: Federal health APIs are modernizing. ClinicalTrials.gov just shipped v2 of their REST API with standardized fields and OpenAPI 3.0 support. The NCI cancer trials API now exposes structured biomarker eligibility data. These APIs didn't exist in this form two years ago. The infrastructure for this project is brand new.

Trend 3: Regulatory pressure to improve trial enrollment — especially for underrepresented populations — is intensifying. The FDA and NIH are actively funding initiatives to make trial matching more systematic and equitable.

Companies like Tempus and Foundation Medicine are already building genomic trial matching at enterprise scale. The difference? Their systems require proprietary genomic data and enterprise EHR integrations. TrialMatch operates entirely on public data, which makes the core logic reproducible, testable, and demonstrable in a portfolio context.

"So it's like building the open-source version of what health-tech companies sell?"

Not quite — it's more focused than that. It's building the reasoning engine that could power such a system, and proving it works with quantitative metrics, a benchmark dataset, structured audit trails, and an honest accounting of its limitations. That's not a demo you wave at during a presentation — it's an engineering artifact that a hiring manager at a health-tech company can actually evaluate, run, and critique.

And honestly? That's the part that excites me most. Not the shiny output — the boring, rigorous, documented proof that the logic holds up under scrutiny. In a world drowning in AI demos that look great and fail quietly, building something that fails loudly and works honestly feels like the most useful thing I could do with a semester project.
